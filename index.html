What is Housing Disrepair?

Housing disrepair means faults or damage in a rented property that makes it unsafe, unhealthy, or unsuitable to live in. Under UK law, landlords are legally required to maintain their properties in a good state of repair and ensure they are safe for tenants.

The most common forms of housing disrepair include:

  • Damp and Mould: Caused by leaks or poor ventilation, leading to health risks such as asthma and respiratory infections.
  • Leaks and Water Damage: Persistent leaks through roofs, walls, or plumbing that can cause structural damage.
  • Heating and Hot Water Failures: Broken boilers or radiators leaving tenants without adequate heating.
  • Electrical Hazards: Faulty wiring, exposed cables, or unsafe sockets that increase the risk of fire or electric shock.
  • Structural Defects: Cracks in walls or ceilings, unstable flooring, or subsidence threatening building integrity.
  • Pest Infestations: Rodents, cockroaches, or other pests due to poor building maintenance.
  • Broken Windows and Doors: Affecting security and insulation.

If you live with any of these issues and your landlord has failed to fix them after you reported them, you may have grounds to make a housing disrepair claim.

Your Legal Rights as a Tenant Facing Housing Disrepair

UK law protects tenants from living in substandard housing. Important legislation includes:

  • Landlord and Tenant Act 1985: Requires landlords to keep the structure and exterior of the property, including installations such as plumbing and heating, in good repair.
  • Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018: Ensures rented properties are safe, healthy, and free from serious hazards.
  • Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS): Used by local councils to assess and enforce housing standards and hazards.
  • Gas Safety (Installation and Use) Regulations 1998: Requires annual safety checks of gas appliances by registered engineers.
  • Electrical Safety Standards 2020: Mandates regular electrical inspections in rented homes to ensure safety.

If your landlord fails to comply with these duties and neglects repairs after you notify them, you have legal grounds to pursue a claim.

Who Can Make a Housing Disrepair Claim?

Generally, you may qualify to make a housing disrepair claim if:

  • You rent your home from a private landlord, local council, or housing association.
  • You have informed your landlord or managing agent of the disrepair problems, ideally in writing.
  • Your landlord has failed to carry out repairs within a reasonable period.
  • You have experienced health issues, injury, or significant inconvenience as a result of the disrepair.

Even if your landlord denies responsibility, legal experts can help gather evidence such as correspondence, medical records, and expert surveys to support your claim.

Common Causes and Warning Signs of Housing Disrepair

Knowing the common causes and warning signs of housing disrepair can help you spot issues early and take prompt action:

  • Neglected Maintenance: Landlords failing to fix leaks, service boilers, or maintain drainage systems.
  • Weather Damage: Storms causing roof damage or blocked gutters leading to water ingress.
  • Poor Ventilation: Resulting in condensation and mould growth.
  • Wear and Tear: Natural aging of property components without timely repair.
  • Structural Problems: Subsidence, cracks, or foundation issues.
  • Pest Infestations: Rodents or insects due to gaps, poor sanitation, or broken windows.

Signs to watch for include:

  • Damp patches or black mould on walls and ceilings
  • Leaking taps, pipes, or ceilings
  • Faulty heating or hot water systems
  • Cracks or bulges in walls and floors
  • Flickering or faulty electrics
  • Persistent pest problems

Keep detailed records including photographs and notes on when issues occur. This will support your claim and help hold your landlord accountable.

What Compensation Can You Receive?

Successful housing disrepair claims may entitle you to various forms of compensation, including:

  • Inconvenience and Distress: Financial redress for living in uncomfortable, hazardous conditions.
  • Health-Related Claims: Compensation if disrepair has caused or worsened illness or injury.
  • Damage to Personal Belongings: If your possessions were harmed by leaks, damp, or pests.
  • Temporary Accommodation Costs: If you had to relocate during urgent repairs.
  • Loss of Enjoyment: Payment for diminished use and enjoyment of your home.

The amount awarded varies case-by-case, depending on the severity and duration of the disrepair and its impacts on your wellbeing.

What Steps Should You Take If You Have Housing Disrepair?

If you notice problems in your home, taking the right steps quickly can protect your health and strengthen your claim:

  1. Report the Issue in Writing: Email or send a letter to your landlord or managing agent describing the problem clearly. Keep a copy for your records.
  2. Document Everything: Take dated photos or videos of the disrepair. Keep notes on how it affects your living conditions and health.
  3. Allow a Reasonable Time to Repair: Typically, 14-28 days depending on the urgency of the issue.
  4. Follow Up if No Action is Taken: Send reminders and keep all correspondence.
  5. Contact Us to Start a Claim: If your landlord fails to act, use our free claim check to begin.

Acting promptly will help protect your rights and increase your chances of success.

Challenges You May Face During Housing Disrepair Claims

  • Repair Delays: Landlords stalling or providing inadequate fixes to avoid legal action.

Housing Disrepair Questions & Answers

Can private tenants make housing disrepair claims?

Yes, private tenants and social housing tenants alike have the right to make claims if their landlord fails to maintain the property in good repair and this causes problems.

How long does a housing disrepair claim usually take?

Claims vary depending on complexity and landlord cooperation, but most cases resolve within 6 to 12 months.

Will I need to attend court for my claim?

Most claims settle out of court. Your solicitor will advise if court action is necessary.

Is there a limit to how much compensation I can claim?

Compensation varies based on the severity, duration, and impact of the disrepair. Awards range from hundreds to tens of thousands of pounds.

What if my landlord refuses to fix the problem?

Your solicitor can take legal steps to compel repairs and claim damages on your behalf.

Do I have to prove that the disrepair caused me harm?

While not always necessary, medical evidence or expert reports strengthen your case significantly.

Can I claim if I rent from a council or housing association?

Yes, tenants of local authorities and housing associations have the same rights to claim as private tenants.

What if I need to move out during repairs?

You may be entitled to compensation for temporary accommodation costs and the inconvenience caused.

Can making a claim affect my tenancy?

It is unlawful for landlords to retaliate against tenants for making claims, though some difficulties may occur. Legal advice is essential to protect your tenancy rights.
