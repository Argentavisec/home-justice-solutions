What Is Housing Disrepair?

Housing disrepair refers to the poor condition of a rental property that your landlord has failed to fix. Under UK housing law, your landlord is required to carry out timely repairs for issues that affect your health, safety, or comfort. Common examples of disrepair include:

  • Black mould and damp due to poor ventilation or leaks
  • Cracked or broken windows, doors, or roofing
  • Faulty heating systems, radiators, or boilers
  • Leaks from pipes, ceilings, or roofs
  • Unsafe electrics, exposed wires, or broken sockets
  • Pest or rodent infestations (rats, mice, cockroaches)
  • Structural problems like damaged walls or floors

If you’ve reported these issues to your landlord and they haven’t resolved them within a reasonable time, you may be eligible to file a housing disrepair claim.

Who Qualifies for a Disrepair Claim?

You could qualify if you meet the following conditions:

  • You rent your home from a council or housing association
  • You have reported the issue to your landlord (verbally or in writing)
  • The landlord failed to resolve the problem within a reasonable time
  • You’ve suffered from poor living conditions, illness, or inconvenience

Compensation can range from £1,000 to £10,000 or more, depending on the severity and duration of the issue. Claims can also include court-mandated repairs and a formal assessment of your home by a qualified expert.

Know Your Rights as a Tenant

Under the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985 and the Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018, your landlord must ensure your home is:

  • Structurally sound and free from hazards
  • Properly ventilated with no damp or mould
  • Free from electrical or gas safety issues
  • Fitted with a functioning heating and hot water system

If these conditions are not met, and your landlord fails to act after being notified, you have the legal right to take action. Visit our Your Rights page for more information.
